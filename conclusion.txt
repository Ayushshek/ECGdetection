Conclusion

This project compared CNN and LSTM autoencoders for reconstruction-based anomaly detection on ECG heartbeat signals from the PTB Diagnostic dataset.

Across all configurations tested, the CNN autoencoder significantly outperformed the LSTM autoencoder in detecting abnormal heartbeats.
The best CNN configuration achieved an overall error rate of 0.000990, with a near-zero abnormal misclassification rate, demonstrating strong separation between normal and abnormal reconstruction errors. 
In contrast, all LSTM configurations produced overall error rates above 0.74, with abnormal misclassification rates above 0.78, meaning the majority of abnormal samples were incorrectly reconstructed as normal.

The key reason for this performance difference lies in how each architecture processes the signal:

CNNs are highly effective at extracting local spatial patterns using convolutional filters. 
ECG heartbeats contain localized waveform structures (P wave, QRS complex, T wave), and convolutional layers are well-suited to learning these short-range morphological features.

LSTMs, while powerful for long-term temporal dependencies, may not be optimal for fixed-length heartbeat segments where local waveform shape is more important than long sequential memory.

Additionally, the CNN architecture introduces fewer sequential dependencies, allowing more stable and efficient learning. 
The LSTM models likely struggled because anomaly detection via reconstruction requires sharp feature compression, and recurrent architectures may blur fine-grained waveform distinctions.

Overall, this experiment demonstrates that for fixed-length ECG heartbeat reconstruction and anomaly detection, CNN-based autoencoders are significantly more effective than LSTM-based autoencoders, particularly when anomalies are characterized by morphological deviations rather than long-range temporal patterns.
